Hormuz crisis grinds on as oil stays elevated and a US Apache crashes near the strait

Facts

  • A US Army Apache attack helicopter crashed near the Strait of Hormuz; President Trump said both crew members were safe. The cause has not been disclosed.
  • Iran has largely blocked Strait of Hormuz shipping since late February, when the US and Israel launched air strikes on Iran. A fragile ceasefire reached in April remains shaky after recent exchanges of fire.
  • Oil remains well above year-ago levels; benchmarks spiked above $100/barrel during the spring escalation, though prices eased after Trump paused a planned strike wave in May.
  • The disruption is feeding higher US gas prices, which AAA pegged near $4.26/gallon in early June, up roughly a third from a year ago.

Left view

Right view

Watch for

Federal judge strikes down Trump's $100,000 H-1B visa fee

Facts

  • US District Judge Leo Sorokin in Boston ruled the $100,000 fee on new H-1B visas an unlawful tax that Congress never authorized.
  • The suit was brought by 20 Democratic state attorneys general; the administration says it will appeal.
  • Employers previously paid roughly $2,000–$5,000 in H-1B fees, depending on circumstances.
  • The fee was central to Trump's effort to curb skilled immigration and boost demand for US workers.

Left view

NPR and the Washington Post cast the ruling as a check on executive overreach, arguing the fee priced out legitimate talent and that the president tried to legislate a tax from the White House.

Right view

Right-leaning coverage (NY Post, Washington Examiner framing) stresses the policy goal—protecting American jobs—and casts the decision as activist judges blocking a lawful immigration-restriction tool the administration will appeal.

Watch for

Appeal likely heads to the First Circuit and possibly the Supreme Court. Tech employers and universities get near-term relief on hiring budgets; watch whether the administration pursues the same end through formal rulemaking instead.

Xi Jinping makes rare Pyongyang visit, courting Kim amid Russia's pull

Facts

  • Xi Jinping visited North Korea June 8–9, his first trip to Pyongyang since 2019 and his first overseas trip of 2026.
  • Xi and Kim Jong Un called for stronger "strategic cooperation"; Xi pitched expanded trade, agriculture, health, and tech ties.
  • The visit coincides with the 65th anniversary of the 1961 China–North Korea mutual defense treaty, Beijing's only formal military alliance.
  • It comes as North Korea has deepened ties with Russia, reportedly sending troops to aid Moscow's war in Ukraine.

Left view

The Guardian and NPR read the trip as Beijing reasserting leverage over a partner drifting toward Moscow, and as evidence US-led isolation of Pyongyang has pushed the autocracies closer together.

Right view

The Washington Times and similar outlets frame it as an authoritarian axis hardening—China, Russia, and North Korea aligning against US interests—and a warning that Washington needs firmer deterrence in the Pacific.

Watch for

Analysts will watch whether China resumes large-scale economic support for the North, any signals on denuclearization talks, and how Beijing balances Pyongyang against its trade relationship with Washington and Seoul.
